  • sequences
    A sequence refers to a set of items, numbers, or events arranged in a specific order



  • drag to container
    It involves dragging objects, such as words, pictures, or shapes, into specific "containers" or areas on the screen




  • find the match
    is a simple and fun activity where you pair two related items, like matching words to pictures or questions to answers. It' s great for learning and reviewing concepts.




  • construct sentence




    • is an activity or game where you solve a problem by fitting pieces together or solving clues. Puzzles are excellent for developing critical thinking, problem-solving, and cognitive skills.





  • family fued
    is a popular game show that can be adapted into a fun classroom activity to encourage team collaboration and critical thinking.
    1.Divide the Class into Two Teams: You can create teams of 3-5 students each. Teams take turns guessing the most popular answers to a question.
    2.Ask Survey Questions: These can be related to the lesson topic (e.g., vocabulary, history, math). For example, if you're teaching a lesson on animals, a question could be, "Name an animal you might see on a farm."
    3.Answering the Questions: The teams take turns guessing the answers to the question. If the answer is on the board, the team gets points based on the popularity of that answer.
    4.Winning the Game: After a set number of rounds, the team with the most points wins.




  • charades
    One student acts out a word or phrase without speaking, and their team tries to guess what it is.



  • hot potato
    Students pass an object (the "hot potato") around while music plays. When the music stops, the student holding the object must answer a question.



  • word ladder
    Give students a starting word, and they must change one letter at a time to form a new word. Each word must be a valid word, and they aim to reach a target word.



  • cross words
    is a word game that consists of a grid of squares, where clues are provided for both horizontal and vertical words. The objective is to fill in the grid with words based on these clues. The words intersect at certain points, which means that letters in one word will help you guess the letters in the other intersecting word. The clues for the words are typically divided into two categories: Across: These are the clues for words that run horizontally. Down: These are the clues for words that run vertically. Each square in the grid can hold one letter, and the answers are typically regular words, sometimes with a theme tying the puzzle together.



  • pictionary
    In this digital version of the classic game, students draw a word or concept on the iPad while others try to guess what it is. It can be customized to include subjectspecific terms.



  • mad libs
    Students fill in the blanks in a story with different types of words (e.g., nouns, verbs, adjectives) to create a humorous or silly version of a story. It’s a great way to reinforce grammar and vocabulary.



  • the yes no game
    The Yes-No Game is a simple and fun conversational game in which one person asks questions, and the other person must respond without saying "yes" or "no." The objective is to answer as creatively and tactfully as possible, avoiding those two words. It can be challenging and humorous as participants try to avoid the obvious responses while still giving an answer. Here’s how it works: One player asks questions to the other player. The second player must respond without using "yes" or "no." If the second player says "yes" or "no," they lose the round, and the game can start again with a new questioner. Example: Question: "Do you like pizza?" Answer: "I definitely enjoy it!"
